Sound

defination of sound

Sound is a form of energy that is produced due to the vibration of an object. When sound waves travel through a medium, they can reflect, refract or diffract depending upon the nature of the medium.

Reflection of Sound

Reflection of sound is the phenomenon of bouncing back of sound waves when they strike a surface. The angle of incidence and the angle of reflection are always equal and they lie in the same plane. This law of reflection of sound is similar to the law of reflection of light.

Echoes

  • defination of echoes

    Echoes are the sounds that we hear when sound waves reflect off a surface and return to our ears after some time delay. Echoes are formed due to the reflection of sound waves from hard surfaces such as walls, cliffs, mountains, etc. The time delay between the original sound and the echo depends on the distance between the sound source and the reflecting surface.

  • Uses of Echoes

    Echoes have several practical applications. They are used by people to determine the distance of an object, location of an object, depth of a sea, etc. For example, the depth of a sea can be determined by sending sound waves from a ship towards the sea bed and measuring the time taken for the echo to return.

Numerical problems on Echoes:

  1. A man standing near a cliff produces a sound and hears the first echo after 3 seconds. He hears the second echo after 2 seconds. Find the distance of the man from the cliff.

    Solution:  Let the distance between the man and the cliff be x.
    The time taken for the sound to travel from the man to the cliff and back to the man (first echo) is 3 seconds.
    The time taken for the sound to travel from the man to the cliff and back to the man (second echo) is 2 seconds.

    Using the formula, distance = speed × time,
    Distance traveled by sound in 3 seconds = 2x
    Distance traveled by sound in 2 seconds = x

    Therefore, the difference in distance covered by the sound between the two echoes = 2x - x = x
    Since the sound travels at a speed of 340 m/s in air, the distance traveled by sound in x time is 340x.

    Hence, 340x = x, which implies x = 170 meters.
    Therefore, the man is 170 meters away from the cliff.


  2. A sound is produced at a distance of 90 meters from a cliff. If the echo is heard after 3 seconds, find the speed of sound.

    Solution:  Let the speed of sound be v.
    The distance traveled by sound to the cliff and back is 2 × 90 = 180 meters.
    The time taken for the sound to travel this distance is 3 seconds.

    Using the formula, distance = speed × time, v × 3 = 180

    Therefore, the speed of sound is v = 180/3 = 60 meters per second.

    Hence, the speed of sound is 60 meters per second.

production of Echoes

Echoes are produced when a sound wave is reflected off a surface and returns back to the listener's ear. The production of echoes is dependent on several factors, including the intensity, frequency, and direction of the sound wave, as well as the nature of the reflecting surface and the distance between the source and the reflecting surface.
The conditions for the formation of echoes are:
  1. Presence of a reflecting surface: For an echo to be produced, there must be a surface that can reflect the sound waves. This can be any surface, such as a wall, mountain, or even the ground.
  2. Distance between source and reflecting surface: The distance between the source of the sound wave and the reflecting surface must be such that the reflected sound wave reaches the listener's ear after a noticeable delay. This delay is what creates the echo effect.
  3. Intensity of the sound wave: The intensity of the original sound wave must be high enough to create a noticeable echo. If the sound wave is too weak, the reflected sound wave will be too weak to be heard.
  4. Direction of the sound wave: The direction of the original sound wave and the direction of the reflected sound wave must be such that they create a noticeable echo. If the reflected sound wave is directed away from the listener, it will not create an echo.
  5. Absence of background noise: The absence of background noise is essential for the formation of clear echoes. If there is too much background noise, the echo may be masked or difficult to hear.
Overall, echoes can be produced under a variety of conditions, but they are most noticeable in large open spaces with hard surfaces that can reflect sound waves back to the listener's ear.

Use of echoes by bats, dolphins, fishermen, medical field.

Bats, dolphins, fishermen, and medical professionals all use echoes in their respective fields.

Bats use echoes to navigate and locate prey. They emit high-frequency sound waves and listen for the echoes that bounce back off of objects in their surroundings. By analyzing the echoes, bats can create a mental image of their environment and detect any obstacles or prey.

Dolphins also use echoes for navigation and locating prey. They emit clicks and listen for the echoes that bounce back, allowing them to determine the location, size, and shape of objects in their environment. This ability is known as echolocation.

Fishermen also use echoes to locate schools of fish. Fish finders use sonar technology to emit sound waves into the water and listen for the echoes that bounce back. By analyzing the echoes, fishermen can locate schools of fish and target their catch more efficiently.

In the medical field, ultrasound imaging uses echoes to create images of the internal structures of the body. High-frequency sound waves are emitted into the body and bounce back off of the different organs and tissues. By analyzing the echoes, doctors can create detailed images of the body's internal structures, aiding in diagnosis and treatment. Ultrasound imaging is commonly used during pregnancy to monitor the growth and development of the fetus.

SONAR

SONAR stands for Sound Navigation And Ranging. It is a technique that is used to detect and locate underwater objects using sound waves. SONAR works by emitting sound waves from a device called a transducer, which then travel through the water and bounce off objects in their path. The reflected sound waves, or echoes, are then detected by the same transducer and converted into electrical signals.

The time taken by the sound waves to travel from the transmitter to the object and back to the receiver is used to determine the distance between the SONAR device and the object. This process is similar to the way in which bats use echolocation to locate their prey.

SONAR is used in a variety of applications, including navigation, communication, and detection of underwater objects such as submarines, shipwrecks, and schools of fish. In the medical field, a type of SONAR called ultrasound is used to create images of internal organs and tissues.

The use of SONAR has revolutionized underwater exploration and has become an essential tool for the military, research organizations, and industries such as oil and gas exploration.

Vibrations

Natural Vibrations:
Natural vibrations are the vibrations of an object or a system that occur without any external force being applied to it. These vibrations occur at a certain frequency known as the natural frequency of the system. Natural vibrations can be observed in various objects such as pendulums, tuning forks, and springs.

Damped Vibrations:

Damped vibrations occur when an external force is applied to an object or a system, causing it to vibrate. Unlike natural vibrations, damped vibrations decrease over time due to the dissipation of energy in the form of heat, sound or any other form of energy. This dissipation of energy is known as damping.

There are different types of damping such as viscous damping, coulomb damping, and hysteresis damping. In viscous damping, energy is dissipated due to the friction between the moving parts of the system. In Coulomb damping, energy is dissipated due to the sliding friction between the two surfaces. In hysteresis damping, energy is dissipated due to the internal friction of the material of the system.

In real-world scenarios, damping is important to control the vibrations of machines and structures to ensure their smooth and safe operation. For example, in a car, shock absorbers help in damping the vibrations caused by the movement of the car on a bumpy road. In musical instruments, damping is used to control the resonance and sustain of the notes produced.

Forced vibrations
Forced vibrations occur when an external periodic force is applied to a system, which causes the system to vibrate with the same frequency as the applied force. The frequency of the external force is known as the driving frequency, and the amplitude of the forced vibration depends on the amplitude of the driving force.

Resonance is a special case of forced vibration that occurs when the driving frequency is equal to the natural frequency of the system. In this case, the amplitude of the vibration becomes very large, and the system may even be destroyed if the amplitude becomes too large.

Example
if a child is swinging on a swing, they are creating forced vibrations. If someone comes along and pushes the child at the right frequency, the swing will start to resonate and the child will swing higher and higher. This is because the frequency of the push is the same as the natural frequency of the swing, which causes the amplitude of the swing to increase.

Another example of resonance is the shattering of a glass by a singer. When a singer sings at the natural frequency of a glass, the glass starts vibrating with larger amplitude and may shatter if the amplitude becomes too large.

Resonance is used in many applications, such as tuning musical instruments, radio and television broadcasting, and even in medical imaging techniques like Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI).

Loudness, pitch and quality of sound

Sound is a form of energy that is produced due to the vibration of an object. It is characterized by three main characteristics: loudness, pitch, and quality.

Loudness:

Loudness is the subjective perception of the intensity of sound. It is the measure of the degree of sensation produced in the ear by the sound wave. Loudness is determined by the amplitude or intensity of the sound wave. The greater the amplitude of the sound wave, the louder the sound will be perceived. The unit of measurement of loudness is the decibel (dB). The human ear can perceive sounds ranging from 0 dB (threshold of hearing) to 140 dB (threshold of pain).

Factors affecting loudness:
 The loudness of a sound depends on the amplitude of the sound wave, the distance from the source of the sound, and the sensitivity of the ear.

Pitch:

Pitch is the subjective perception of the frequency of sound. It is the measure of the highness or lowness of a sound. Pitch is determined by the frequency of the sound wave. The higher the frequency of the sound wave, the higher the pitch will be perceived. The unit of measurement of pitch is hertz (Hz). The range of audible frequencies for the human ear is from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.

Factors affecting pitch:
The pitch of a sound depends on the frequency of the sound wave and the sensitivity of the ear.

Quality:

Quality is the subjective perception of the complexity of sound. It is the measure of the purity or richness of a sound. Quality is determined by the harmonic content of the sound wave. The more the number of harmonics in the sound wave, the richer the quality of the sound will be perceived.

Factors affecting quality:
The quality of a sound depends on the number and intensity of the harmonics in the sound wave.

In summary, loudness is the measure of the degree of sensation produced in the ear by the sound wave, pitch is the measure of the highness or lowness of a sound, and quality is the measure of the purity or richness of a sound. The factors affecting loudness, pitch, and quality are mainly related to the amplitude, frequency, and harmonic content of the sound wave, as well as the sensitivity of the ear and the distance from the source of the sound.
